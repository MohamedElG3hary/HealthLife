    1. What is Body Mass Index (BMI)?

    • Body Mass Index (BMI) is a tool : used to assess weight in relation to height and determine whether a person is underweight, has a normal weight, is overweight, or is obese. It is calculated using the following formula:
        • BMI is considered a general measure of health, but it does not take into account individual differences such as body composition or muscle-to-fat ratio.

    2. Essential Element Ratios and Their Impact on BMI

    • Fat: A high body fat percentage may lead to an increased BMI, which often indicates overweight or obesity.
    • Muscle:High muscle mass can increase BMI without necessarily implying poor health, as muscles are denser than fat.
    • Bone: Bone mass has a minor impact but can influence the assessment in certain individual cases.

    3. Limitations and Risks of Relying Solely on BMI

    • Although BMI is a useful tool, it does not provide a comprehensive picture of health. For example:
      Athletes may have a high BMI due to muscle mass rather than fat.
    • BMI does not distinguish between healthy fat (such as brown fat) and unhealthy fat (such as visceral fat).
    • It does not consider other factors such as fat distribution or cardiovascular health.
    • Therefore, it is recommended to combine BMI with body composition analysis for a more accurate health assessment.
